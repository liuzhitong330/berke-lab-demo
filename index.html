The Molecular Substrate of the Striatal Reward-Time Gradient

Cathy Liu · cathyliu014@gmail.com · personal exploratory project, July 2026
dopamine striatum Allen Brain Atlas Python

A recent finding from the Berke lab showed that dopamine transients in distinct striatal subregions convey reward prediction errors over distinct time horizons — more ventral regions (nucleus accumbens) signal immediate rewards, while more dorsal regions (caudoputamen) reflect longer time horizons. This is a compelling systems-level result. I got curious about what the molecular underpinning looks like: do the genes that define direct- and indirect-pathway MSNs show a corresponding gradient along the same dorsal–ventral axis?

To check, I pulled in situ hybridization expression data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas for ten dopamine pathway genes across three striatal subregions (caudoputamen → nucleus accumbens → olfactory tubercle), roughly tracing the dorsal-to-ventral axis.

What this is. A personal exploratory project, not novel research. All data is from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas public API (Allen Institute for Brain Science, 2004). Expression values are ISH expression energy (sum of expressing pixels × intensity, normalized by annotation volume). Analysis is mine alone.

Results

What the data shows

Several gradients are visible that fit the reward-gradient story:

  • Drd1 and Drd2 both decline ventrally, but Drd2 drops faster — so the Drd1/Drd2 ratio rises toward the olfactory tubercle (1.97 → 2.16 → 4.20). The olfactory tubercle, the most ventral striatal territory, is heavily biased toward D1-expressing direct-pathway MSNs. Electrophysiologically, this region responds most strongly to immediate reward — consistent with a D1-dominant motivational drive signal.
  • Adora2a (A2aR), an indirect-pathway co-marker, follows Drd2's profile closely — high in dorsal CP, dropping sharply ventrally — supporting the interpretation that indirect-pathway influence is proportionally greater in dorsal regions encoding longer time-horizon prediction errors.
  • Ppp1r1b (DARPP-32) is uniformly high in CP and ACB but drops in the olfactory tubercle, reflecting the latter's distinct MSN composition. Th (tyrosine hydroxylase) peaks in ACB — dopaminergic axons are densest there, consistent with nucleus accumbens being the canonical reward-prediction target.

These molecular gradients don't explain the temporal gradient Berke's lab found at the systems level — that's an emergent circuit property — but they suggest the structural substrate is already encoded at the transcriptional level and could be interrogated in future single-cell or spatial transcriptomic experiments in the striatum.

Why this connects to the lab's work

  • The core question here — how to extract quantitative spatial patterns from large public atlas data — is directly related to computational approaches the Berke lab uses alongside its electrophysiology work. I want to bring that analytical perspective to the lab's datasets.
  • My background is in both wet lab and computation: I've done animal behavior and circuit-level experiments, and I've built data pipelines for multi-modal neuroscience data. That combination is what I'd aim to contribute here.
  • The analyses I find most compelling in the lab's recent papers are those that integrate multiple data modalities — e.g., pairing fast-scan voltammetry with spiking data to decompose the dopamine signal. I'm excited to work on that kind of integration problem.
